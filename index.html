A quick history of photography in Paris from 1830 to 1944

July Revolution

the July Revolution (révolution de Juillet), Second French Revolution or Trois Glorieuses in French ("Three Glorious [Days]"), led to the overthrow of King Charles X, the French Bourbon monarch, and the ascent of his cousin Louis Philippe, Duke of Orléans. It marked the shift from one constitutional monarchy, under the restored House of Bourbon, to another, the July Monarchy; the transition of power from the House of Bourbon to its cadet branch, the House of Orléans; and the replacement of the principle of hereditary right by popular sovereignty. Supporters of the Bourbon would be called Legitimists, and supporters of Louis Philippe Orléanists.

1830

Daguerre in Paris

It is the very fist photograph of Paris, but also the very fist photograph of a human being. It was made by Daguerre himself, the inventor of the daguerrotypes. At that time exposure times were very long, that's why it is impossible to see the trafic. The man must have stayed here for at least 15 minutes in order to be visible.

1839

Le Pont Neuf, Daguerre, 1839

It is the oldest photograph of the oldest bridge in Paris.

1839

Le Louvre, Daguerre, 1839

At that time the Louvre was already a museum, and its famous department of Egyptian antiquities had been open to the public for a few decades.

1839

On this picture, you can see how the cathedral was decorated for the funeral of the Duke of Orélans Ferdinand Philippe, son of king Louis-Philippe. Six years later, his father would be deposed by the Revolution of 1848.

1842

Montmartre, Unknown, 1845

At that time Montmartre was still a separate commune from Paris, famous for its mills.

1845

In 1848 a big uprising in Paris ended the rule of Louis-Philippe and gave birth to the Second Republic. On this picture one can see the barricades of the Saint-Maur street during the revolt.

1848

Beginning of the Second Empire

On 2 December 1851, Louis-Napoléon Bonaparte, who had been elected President of the Republic, staged a coup d'état by dissolving the National Assembly without having the constitutional right to do so. He thus became sole ruler of France, and re-established universal suffrage, previously abolished by the Assembly. His decisions were popularly endorsed by a referendum later that month that attracted an implausible 92 percent support.

1852

Rue des Marmousets, Charles Marville, 1853

In 1853 Napoleon III entrusted Haussmann with the redevelopment of Paris. Indeed, the city still had many small middle-aged streets in which traffic was difficult. It was there that the Paris of the grand boulevards and gardens was born.

1853

Vue panoramique du palais entier et jardin, de la rue de Rivoli aux immeubles du quai de Seine, E. Baldus, around 1855

This is one of the only photos of the Tuileries that we have. The palace was indeed destroyed by the communards in 1873.

1855

Vue du nouveau pont Saint-Michel à Paris, Auguste Hippolyte Collard, 1859

Paris transformations were not only in the streets. Here is a picture of the brand new Saint-Michel bridge. In the background you can see the spire of Notre-Dame in renovation.

1859

La cour impériale à Fontainebleau, Adolphe Braun, 1860

Napoleon III wanted his reign to be that of the revival of France and progress. This is why he was very interested in photography and that he launched the heliographic mission to archive the historic heritage of the country in the form of photos.

1860

Sarah Bernhardt, Nadar, 1864

Nadar was the greatest portraitist of Paris. He took photos of Baudelaire, Hugo, Dumas, Zola... Here is the protrait of Sarah Bernhardt, one of the most important actress of the nineteenth century.

1864

Scherzo di Follia, Pierre-Louis Pierson, around 1865

This portrait of the Countess of Castiglione has become emblematic of the history of photographic art.

1865

Enfants du peuple , Unknown, around 1865

If the Second Empire was quickly developping, it was to the detriment of the working classes whose mortality was breaking records and where children worked from an early age

1865

Beginning of the Third Republic

After the disaster of Sedan during the Franco-Prussian war, Napoleon III was captured by the Prussians and the Third Republic was proclamed in Paris. The new government decided to surrender tu Prussia. In Paris, the population that was under siege for four months rebelled and proclamed Paris Commune. However, the rebellion was crushed in blood on May 28, 1871.

1870

Barricades près du Ministère de la Marine et l'Hôtel Crillon, Auguste Hippolyte Collard, 1871

The communards, whose main leaders were socialists, erected barricades all over Paris to defend themselves against the government of Adolf Thiers.

1871

Palais des Tuileries, Jean-Eugène Durand, 1871

During the insurrection many remarkable constructions were destroyed, but the most famous is the Tuileries palace.

1871

Montage des piliers au-dessus du premier étage, La tour de 300 mètresd, 1888

The Eiffel Tower was built for the Exposition Universelle of 1889. If it was contested at the beginning, it surely became the most famous symbol of the city.

1888

Paris : rue de Rivoli, Eugène Trutat, 1899

The Parisian metropolitan was started at the turn of the nineteenth century. If it was supposed to be in function for the Exposition universelle of 1900, the first line was operational three month later.

1899

Le Moulin Rouge, Unknown, 1900

At the end of the century, the culture of cabaret emerged in Paris and famous stars like Gaby Deslys or Mistinguett created spectacles that gathered thousands of Parisians and strangers every nights.

1900

Inondations de Paris janvier 1910, Agence Rol, 1910

In 1010 happened the Great Flood of Paris in which the Seine River, carrying winter rains from its tributaries, flooded the Paris conurbation, France. The Seine water level rose eight metres above the ordinary level.

1910

Portrait de famille, Stéphane Passet, 1914

This colored autochrome depicts a typical Parisian family of the beginning of the century. It was taken for the World Archives project of Albert Khan.

1914

Beginning of World War I

After the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria, the complex interplay of alliances and France's desire for revenge on Germany for the humiliation of 1870 led France to get involved in what would become the First World War.

1914

Poilus en permission, Gallica, 1916

During there permissions, Parisian soldiers often returned to Paris to rest and have fun at the cabaret.

1916

Café «Le Franc Buveur», rue Saint-Rustique, Roger Viollet, 1921

After WWI began the "Années folles" in Paris. A great cultural revival is taking place in the cafes of Montmartre and Montparnasse. Cars take over the streets and young people who want to forget the war are unleashed in cabarets.

1921

Josephine Baker, Walery, 1927

During the Année folles women like Josephine Baker or Kiki de Montparnasse ran cabarets and performed there every night in front of people like Cocteau, Modigliani, Picasso, Coco Chanel ...

1927

Manifestation du 14 juillet 1935, Paris, place de la Bastille,agence Meurisse, 1935

After the Années folles, the crisis of 1929 calmed the heat and helped the rise of nationalisms in Germany in Italy and in Spain. It is in this context that the different currents of the French left allied in the Popular Front and we conquered power. They have implemented famous measures such as paid leave.

1935

les Parisiens découvrent les affiches annonçant l'ordre de mobilisation générale. ,Unknown, 1939

Tensions with Germany only increased after the Popular Front episode. And, despite attempts to preserve peace and the French reluctance to war, general mobilization was decreed in 1939.

1939

Beginning of World War II

After the rise of Hitler, Germans troops marched on France, which capitulated after the so called "phoney war" and Paris fall in the hand of the Germans.

1939

Hitler in font of the Eiffel Tower, Heinrich Hoffmann, 1940

After the capitulation Hitler came in Paris and his photographer took this picture. The occupation is characterized by scarcity, dictatorship of the occupier and the roundups of Jews, like the one of the Vel d'Hiv.

1940

Pierre Georges, Gestapo, 1941

During the occupation, the Resistance was strongly implemented in Paris, were the siege of the CNR (Conseil National de la Resistance) was located 48 rue du Four. Pierre George was the first Resistant to attack a German officer in Paris, at the metro station Barbes.

1941

The Liberation of Paris, Unknown, 1944

After four years of occupation, Paris was finally liberated by the Allies.
