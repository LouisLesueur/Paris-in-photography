A quick history of photography in Paris

July Revolution

the July Revolution (révolution de Juillet), Second French Revolution or Trois Glorieuses in French ("Three Glorious [Days]"), led to the overthrow of King Charles X, the French Bourbon monarch, and the ascent of his cousin Louis Philippe, Duke of Orléans. It marked the shift from one constitutional monarchy, under the restored House of Bourbon, to another, the July Monarchy; the transition of power from the House of Bourbon to its cadet branch, the House of Orléans; and the replacement of the principle of hereditary right by popular sovereignty. Supporters of the Bourbon would be called Legitimists, and supporters of Louis Philippe Orléanists.

Daguerre in Paris

Le Boulevard du Temple, Daguerre, 1839

It is the very fist photograph of Paris, but also the very fist photograph of a human being. At that time exposure times were very long, that's why it is impossible to see the trafic. The guy must have stayed here for at least 15 minutes in order to be visible.

1839

Le Pont Neuf, Daguerre, 1839

It is the oldest photograph of the oldest bridge in Paris.

1839

Le Louvre, Daguerre, 1839

At that time the Louvre was already a museum, and its famous department of Egyptian antiquities had been open to the public for a few decades.

1839

La rive gauche, Unknown, 1840

On this impressive panorama of the left bank of the Seine, one can see the Quais Conti in its period condition, without masonry.

1840

Notre-Dame, Unknown, 1840

On this picture you can see Notre-Dame before its restoration by Viollet le Duc. The spire had been missing since the first Revolution.

1840

Funérailles du Duc D’Orléans, Unknown, 1842

On this picture, you can see how the cathedral was decorated for the funeral of the Duke of Orélans Ferdinand Philippe, son of king Louis-Philippe. Six years later, his father would be deposed by the Revolution of 1848.

1842

Montmartre, Unknown, 1845

At that time Montmartre was still a separate commune from Paris, famous for its mills.

1845

Barricades de la rue Saint-Maur, Unknown, 1848

In 1848 a big uprising in Paris ended the rule of Louis-Philippe and gave birth to the Second Republic. On this picture one can see the barricades of the Saint-Maur street during the revolt.
